Understanding Food Allergies and Intolerances

April 19, 2022 · 14 min read

Okay, let's dive into a topic that affects a lot of people: food allergies and intolerances. You might experience bloating after eating certain foods, or maybe a rash appears. Is it an allergy? Is it an intolerance? What's the difference? This article will break it all down in a clear and easy-to-understand way.

What's the Big Difference? Allergy vs. Intolerance​

The core difference lies in how your body reacts.

  • Food Allergy: This involves your immune system. Your body mistakenly identifies a food protein as a threat and launches an attack. This can trigger a range of symptoms, some of which can be life-threatening. Even a tiny amount of the offending food can cause a reaction.

  • Food Intolerance: This usually involves your digestive system. Your body has difficulty digesting a specific food. It doesn't involve the immune system in the same way an allergy does. The symptoms are generally less severe than an allergic reaction, and you might be able to tolerate small amounts of the food.

Think of it this way: a food allergy is like a full-blown military assault on a harmless invader (the food protein). A food intolerance is more like a traffic jam in your digestive system – uncomfortable, but not life-threatening (usually!).

Food Allergies: When Your Immune System Overreacts​

A food allergy happens when your immune system sees a particular food (or a substance in that food, usually a protein) as a danger. It then creates antibodies (specifically, IgE antibodies) to "fight" this food. The next time you eat that food, these antibodies trigger the release of chemicals like histamine, which cause the allergic symptoms.

Common Food Allergens​

While you can be allergic to practically any food, some are much more common than others. These are often referred to as the "Big 9" food allergens in the United States:

  1. Milk: Often affects children, but many outgrow it.
  2. Eggs: Another common childhood allergy.
  3. Peanuts: A very common and often severe allergy.
  4. Tree Nuts: Includes almonds, walnuts, pecans, cashews, etc. People are often allergic to multiple types of tree nuts.
  5. Soy: Found in many processed foods.
  6. Wheat: Not the same as gluten intolerance (more on that later).
  7. Fish: Common allergies include salmon, tuna, and cod.
  8. Shellfish: Includes shrimp, crab, lobster, and clams.
  9. Sesame: Increasingly recognized as a common allergen.

Symptoms of a Food Allergy​

Allergy symptoms can range from mild to severe, and they can appear within minutes to a couple of hours after eating the offending food. Here's a breakdown of potential symptoms:

  • Skin Reactions: Hives (itchy, raised welts), eczema (itchy, inflamed skin), itching, swelling.
  • Gastrointestinal Issues: Nausea, vomiting, diarrhea, abdominal pain, cramping.
  • Respiratory Problems: Wheezing, coughing, shortness of breath, runny nose, nasal congestion, throat tightness, difficulty breathing.
  • Cardiovascular Issues: Dizziness, lightheadedness, fainting.
  • Anaphylaxis: This is a severe, life-threatening allergic reaction. It can involve multiple systems in the body and can cause a sudden drop in blood pressure, difficulty breathing, loss of consciousness, and even death. Anaphylaxis requires immediate medical attention. Use an epinephrine auto-injector (EpiPen) if you have one, and call emergency services immediately.

Diagnosis of Food Allergies​

If you suspect you have a food allergy, it's crucial to see an allergist (a doctor who specializes in allergies). They can perform tests to help determine if you are truly allergic and to which foods. Common diagnostic methods include:

  • Skin Prick Test: A small amount of the suspected allergen is placed on your skin, and the skin is pricked. If you're allergic, a small, raised bump (hive) will appear.
  • Blood Test (IgE Antibody Test): This test measures the amount of IgE antibodies specific to certain foods in your blood. A high level of these antibodies suggests an allergy.
  • Oral Food Challenge: This is considered the "gold standard" for diagnosing food allergies. Under strict medical supervision, you'll be given small, increasing doses of the suspected allergen to see if you react. This test is only done in a controlled environment where emergency treatment is available.
  • Elimination Diet: Under the guidance of a healthcare professional, you'll remove suspected allergenic foods from your diet for a period of time and then reintroduce them one by one to see if symptoms return.

Management of Food Allergies​

The cornerstone of food allergy management is strict avoidance of the allergenic food. This means carefully reading food labels, asking about ingredients when eating out, and being aware of potential cross-contamination.

  • Read Labels Carefully: Food labels are required to list the "Big 9" allergens. Look for phrases like "Contains: Milk, Eggs, Peanuts..." Be aware of "may contain" statements, which indicate that the food may have been exposed to the allergen during processing.
  • Communicate Clearly: When eating out, inform the restaurant staff about your allergies. Ask how the food is prepared and if there's any risk of cross-contamination.
  • Carry an Epinephrine Auto-Injector (EpiPen): If you have a severe allergy, your doctor will likely prescribe an epinephrine auto-injector. Learn how to use it properly and carry it with you at all times. Make sure your family and friends know how to administer it in case of an emergency.
  • Wear a Medical Alert Bracelet: This can alert emergency personnel to your allergies if you're unable to communicate.
  • Be Aware of Cross-Contamination: Cross-contamination happens when an allergen comes into contact with a food that's supposed to be allergen-free. This can happen on shared cutting boards, utensils, or cooking surfaces.

Cross-Reactivity​

Cross-reactivity occurs when the proteins in one substance (like a pollen) are similar to the proteins in another substance (like a food). This can cause you to have an allergic reaction to the food even if you're not directly allergic to it.

  • Pollen-Food Syndrome (Oral Allergy Syndrome): This is a common example of cross-reactivity. People with pollen allergies (like birch pollen or ragweed pollen) may experience itching or tingling in their mouth after eating certain fruits or vegetables (like apples, peaches, or melons). The proteins in these foods are similar to the proteins in the pollen.

Food Intolerances: Trouble Digesting​

Food intolerances, unlike allergies, don't involve the immune system. Instead, they usually occur because your body lacks the enzymes or other substances needed to properly digest a particular food. The symptoms are generally less severe than those of an allergy, and they often involve the digestive system.

Common Food Intolerances​

Some of the most common food intolerances include:

  1. Lactose Intolerance: This is the inability to digest lactose, a sugar found in milk and dairy products. It's caused by a deficiency of the enzyme lactase, which breaks down lactose in the small intestine.
  2. Gluten Intolerance (Non-Celiac Gluten Sensitivity): This is a reaction to gluten, a protein found in wheat, barley, and rye. It's not the same as celiac disease, which is an autoimmune disorder triggered by gluten. People with non-celiac gluten sensitivity experience symptoms similar to those of celiac disease, but they don't have the same intestinal damage.
  3. Histamine Intolerance: Histamine is a chemical involved in various bodily functions, including immune response and digestion. Some people have difficulty breaking down histamine, leading to a buildup in the body and causing a range of symptoms.
  4. FODMAP Intolerance: FODMAPs (Fermentable Oligosaccharides, Disaccharides, Monosaccharides, and Polyols) are a group of short-chain carbohydrates that are poorly absorbed in the small intestine. They can ferment in the gut, leading to gas, bloating, and other digestive issues.
  5. Caffeine Intolerance: Sensitivity to the stimulant effects of caffeine, leading to anxiety, insomnia, and digestive upset.
  6. Sulfites Intolerance: Reaction to sulfites, preservatives found in wine, dried fruits, and processed foods.
  7. Food Additives and Artificial Sweeteners Intolerance: Sensitivity to artificial colors, flavors, or sweeteners, leading to various symptoms.

Symptoms of Food Intolerance​

Symptoms of food intolerance are often delayed, appearing hours or even days after eating the offending food. They typically involve the digestive system, but can also affect other parts of the body. Common symptoms include:

  • Gastrointestinal Issues: Bloating, gas, abdominal pain, diarrhea, constipation, nausea.
  • Headaches: Migraines or tension headaches.
  • Skin Reactions: Rashes, hives, or eczema (though less common than with allergies).
  • Fatigue: Feeling tired or sluggish.
  • Brain Fog: Difficulty concentrating or remembering things.
  • Joint Pain: Aches and pains in the joints.

Diagnosis of Food Intolerances​

Diagnosing food intolerances can be more challenging than diagnosing food allergies because there are no reliable, standardized tests for many intolerances. Diagnosis often relies on:

  • Elimination Diet: This is the most common method for identifying food intolerances. You'll remove suspected trigger foods from your diet for a period of time (usually 2-6 weeks) and then reintroduce them one by one to see if symptoms return. It's important to work with a registered dietitian or healthcare professional when doing an elimination diet.
  • Food Diary: Keeping a detailed record of what you eat and when, along with any symptoms you experience, can help you identify potential trigger foods.
  • Hydrogen Breath Test: This test is used to diagnose lactose intolerance and small intestinal bacterial overgrowth (SIBO). It measures the amount of hydrogen gas in your breath after consuming lactose. High levels of hydrogen indicate that the lactose is not being properly digested and is being fermented by bacteria in the gut.
  • Stool Tests: These tests can help assess gut health and identify imbalances in gut bacteria, which may contribute to food intolerances.
  • Trial and Error: Sometimes, simply experimenting with your diet and noting how you feel after eating certain foods can help you identify intolerances.

Management of Food Intolerances​

Managing food intolerances usually involves:

  • Avoiding or Limiting Trigger Foods: Once you've identified the foods you're intolerant to, you'll need to avoid them or limit your intake to a level that doesn't cause symptoms.
  • Enzyme Supplements: For some intolerances, such as lactose intolerance, enzyme supplements can help you digest the offending food. Lactase pills, for example, can be taken before consuming dairy products to help break down lactose.
  • Probiotics: Probiotics are beneficial bacteria that can help improve gut health and digestion. They may be helpful for managing some food intolerances, particularly those related to gut imbalances.
  • Dietary Changes: Following a specific diet, such as a low-FODMAP diet, can help manage symptoms of certain food intolerances.
  • Lifestyle Modifications: Managing stress, getting enough sleep, and staying hydrated can also help improve digestive health and reduce symptoms of food intolerance.

Celiac Disease: A Different Kind of Reaction to Gluten​

It's important to distinguish gluten intolerance (non-celiac gluten sensitivity) from celiac disease. Celiac disease is an autoimmune disorder in which gluten triggers an immune response that damages the small intestine. This damage can interfere with the absorption of nutrients and lead to a variety of health problems.

Symptoms of Celiac Disease​

Celiac disease can cause a wide range of symptoms, including:

  • Digestive Issues: Diarrhea, abdominal pain, bloating, gas, constipation, nausea, vomiting.
  • Malabsorption: Weight loss, anemia, fatigue, nutrient deficiencies.
  • Skin Rashes: Dermatitis herpetiformis (a blistering, itchy skin rash).
  • Neurological Symptoms: Headaches, fatigue, brain fog, peripheral neuropathy.
  • Other Symptoms: Bone pain, joint pain, infertility, mouth ulcers.

Diagnosis of Celiac Disease​

Celiac disease is diagnosed through:

  • Blood Tests: These tests look for specific antibodies, such as anti-tissue transglutaminase (anti-tTG) and anti-endomysial antibodies (EMA).
  • Endoscopy with Biopsy: A small sample of tissue is taken from the small intestine and examined under a microscope to look for damage.

Treatment of Celiac Disease​

The only treatment for celiac disease is a strict gluten-free diet for life. This means completely avoiding all foods containing wheat, barley, and rye. It's essential to work with a registered dietitian to ensure you're getting all the necessary nutrients while following a gluten-free diet.

Histamine Intolerance: When Your Body Can't Break Down Histamine​

Histamine is a chemical involved in many bodily functions, including immune response, digestion, and neurotransmission. Some people have difficulty breaking down histamine, leading to a buildup in the body and causing a range of symptoms. This is known as histamine intolerance.

Causes of Histamine Intolerance​

Histamine intolerance can be caused by:

  • DAO Deficiency: Diamine oxidase (DAO) is an enzyme that breaks down histamine in the gut. A deficiency in DAO can lead to histamine buildup.
  • Excess Histamine Production: Some conditions, such as small intestinal bacterial overgrowth (SIBO), can lead to increased histamine production in the gut.
  • Histamine-Releasing Foods: Certain foods can trigger the release of histamine in the body.
  • Medications: Some medications can interfere with DAO activity or increase histamine levels.

Symptoms of Histamine Intolerance​

Symptoms of histamine intolerance can vary widely and may include:

  • Skin Reactions: Hives, itching, flushing, eczema.
  • Gastrointestinal Issues: Diarrhea, abdominal pain, bloating, nausea, vomiting.
  • Headaches: Migraines or tension headaches.
  • Respiratory Problems: Nasal congestion, runny nose, sneezing, asthma.
  • Cardiovascular Issues: Rapid heartbeat, low blood pressure, dizziness.
  • Other Symptoms: Anxiety, fatigue, insomnia, menstrual problems.

Diagnosis of Histamine Intolerance​

Diagnosing histamine intolerance can be challenging. There is no single, reliable test. Diagnosis often involves:

  • Elimination Diet: Following a low-histamine diet for a period of time and then reintroducing foods one by one to see if symptoms return.
  • DAO Blood Test: Measuring DAO levels in the blood. However, this test is not always accurate.
  • Symptom Tracking: Keeping a detailed record of symptoms and food intake.

Management of Histamine Intolerance​

Managing histamine intolerance involves:

  • Low-Histamine Diet: Avoiding or limiting high-histamine foods, such as fermented foods (cheese, yogurt, sauerkraut), aged meats, alcohol, and certain fruits and vegetables (tomatoes, spinach, eggplant).
  • DAO Supplements: Taking DAO supplements before meals to help break down histamine in the gut.
  • Antihistamines: Taking antihistamines to block the effects of histamine.
  • Treating Underlying Conditions: Addressing any underlying conditions that may be contributing to histamine intolerance, such as SIBO.

The Importance of Seeking Professional Advice​

It's essential to consult with a healthcare professional, such as a doctor, allergist, or registered dietitian, if you suspect you have a food allergy or intolerance. Self-diagnosing and self-treating can be dangerous and may lead to nutrient deficiencies or other health problems. A healthcare professional can help you:

  • Accurately diagnose your condition.
  • Develop a safe and effective management plan.
  • Identify and avoid trigger foods.
  • Ensure you're getting all the necessary nutrients.
  • Monitor your progress and make adjustments to your plan as needed.

Key Takeaways:​

  • Food allergies involve the immune system, while food intolerances usually involve the digestive system.
  • Food allergy symptoms can range from mild to life-threatening (anaphylaxis).
  • Food intolerance symptoms are generally less severe and often involve the digestive system.
  • Diagnosis of food allergies involves skin prick tests, blood tests, and oral food challenges.
  • Diagnosis of food intolerances often relies on elimination diets and symptom tracking.
  • Management of food allergies involves strict avoidance of the allergenic food.
  • Management of food intolerances involves avoiding or limiting trigger foods and making dietary changes.
  • Celiac disease is an autoimmune disorder triggered by gluten, while gluten intolerance is a sensitivity to gluten without intestinal damage.
  • Histamine intolerance is caused by a buildup of histamine in the body and can be managed with a low-histamine diet and DAO supplements.
  • It's crucial to seek professional advice from a healthcare professional for diagnosis and management of food allergies and intolerances.

Understanding the difference between food allergies and intolerances is essential for managing your health and well-being. By paying attention to your body, working with healthcare professionals, and making informed choices about your diet, you can live a happy and healthy life, even with food sensitivities. Remember to always prioritize your health and seek guidance from qualified professionals for personalized advice and treatment.
