The Essential Guide to Reading Food Labels Like a Pro

April 16, 2021 · 11 min read

The Essential Guide to Reading Food Labels Like a Pro​

Ever feel lost staring at a food label? You're not alone! Those tiny numbers and unfamiliar ingredients can seem like a foreign language. But fear not! This guide will equip you with the knowledge to read food labels like a pro, empowering you to make healthier choices for yourself and your family.

Why Bother Reading Food Labels?​

Think of food labels as your personal cheat sheet to a healthier you. They provide vital information about the nutritional content of packaged foods, helping you:

  • Make Informed Choices: Compare different products and select those that align with your dietary needs and goals.
  • Control Your Intake: Track your calorie, fat, sugar, and sodium consumption.
  • Identify Hidden Ingredients: Uncover potentially harmful additives, allergens, or unhealthy fats.
  • Improve Your Overall Health: By understanding what you're eating, you can make better choices that contribute to a healthier lifestyle.
  • Manage Specific Health Conditions: Food labels are crucial for managing conditions like diabetes, high blood pressure, or food allergies.

Decoding the Nutrition Facts Label​

The Nutrition Facts label is your primary source of information. Let's break down each section:

1. Serving Size: The Foundation of Everything​

This is arguably the MOST important part of the label. Why? Because all the nutritional information that follows is based on this serving size.

  • What it is: The serving size is the amount of food that is typically eaten at one time. It's listed in common household measures (e.g., 1 cup, 2 cookies, 1 slice).
  • Why it matters: If you eat more than the serving size, you're consuming more calories, fat, sugar, and everything else listed. If you eat less, you're consuming less.
  • Example: The label might say "Serving Size: 1 cup." If you eat 2 cups, you need to double all the values on the label.
  • Pay Attention to Servings Per Container: This tells you how many servings are in the entire package. A single-serving bag of chips is easy to understand, but a large bag might contain multiple servings, leading to overconsumption if you're not careful.
  • Realistic Serving Sizes: Serving sizes aren't necessarily recommended serving sizes. They are based on what people typically eat. Be realistic about how much you eat and adjust your calculations accordingly. Manufacturers have been trying to make serving sizes more realistic to reflect how much people generally eat.

2. Calories: Energy In, Energy Out​

  • What it is: Calories measure the energy you get from a serving of food.
  • Why it matters: Calories are essential for life, but consuming too many can lead to weight gain.
  • General Guidelines:
    • 40 calories is low
    • 100 calories is moderate
    • 400 calories or more is high
  • Your Individual Needs: Calorie needs vary based on age, sex, activity level, and individual metabolism. Use online calculators or consult a registered dietitian to determine your daily calorie needs.
  • Calories from Fat: While this used to be a separate line, it's now part of the total Calories count, making the label less confusing. Focus on the types of fat, not just the calories from fat.

3. Total Fat: Understanding the Types​

  • What it is: The total amount of fat in a serving.
  • Why it matters: Fat is essential, but some types are healthier than others.
  • Saturated Fat: Limit your intake of saturated fat, as it can raise LDL ("bad") cholesterol levels and increase the risk of heart disease. Aim for less than 10% of your daily calories from saturated fat.
  • Trans Fat: Avoid trans fat completely. It's artificially produced and has no known health benefits. The FDA has taken steps to remove trans fats from the food supply, but it's still important to check labels. Look for "partially hydrogenated oil" in the ingredients list – that's trans fat in disguise. Even if the label says "0g Trans Fat," it can contain up to 0.5 grams per serving. If you eat multiple servings, it can add up.
  • Unsaturated Fat: Focus on getting more unsaturated fats, including monounsaturated and polyunsaturated fats. These fats are beneficial for heart health. Sources include olive oil, avocados, nuts, seeds, and fatty fish (like salmon).
  • Cholesterol: Cholesterol is also listed under total fat. Dietary cholesterol has less of an impact on blood cholesterol than saturated and trans fats do. But, if you have high cholesterol, it's still wise to limit your intake.
  • Fat-Free Does Not Mean Healthy: Be wary of products labeled "fat-free." Often, manufacturers add sugar, salt, or other unhealthy ingredients to compensate for the lack of fat.

4. Sodium: Watch Your Salt Intake​

  • What it is: The amount of sodium (salt) in a serving.
  • Why it matters: Excessive sodium intake can raise blood pressure and increase the risk of heart disease and stroke.
  • Recommended Daily Intake: The Dietary Guidelines for Americans recommend limiting sodium intake to less than 2,300 milligrams per day.
  • Hidden Sources of Sodium: Processed foods, canned goods, frozen meals, and restaurant foods are often high in sodium.
  • Sodium-Free, Low Sodium, Reduced Sodium:
    • Sodium-Free: Less than 5 mg of sodium per serving.
    • Very Low Sodium: 35 mg or less of sodium per serving.
    • Low Sodium: 140 mg or less of sodium per serving.
    • Reduced Sodium: At least 25% less sodium than the regular product.
  • Taste Buds Adapt: Reducing your sodium intake gradually can help your taste buds adjust, making it easier to enjoy foods with less salt.

5. Total Carbohydrate: Fueling Your Body​

  • What it is: The total amount of carbohydrates in a serving, including fiber, sugars, and starches.
  • Why it matters: Carbohydrates are your body's primary source of energy.
  • Fiber: Aim for a high fiber intake. Fiber promotes digestive health, helps you feel full, and can lower cholesterol levels. Look for foods with at least 3 grams of fiber per serving.
  • Sugars: Pay close attention to the amount of added sugars. These are sugars that are added to foods during processing. The Nutrition Facts label now distinguishes between total sugars and added sugars.
  • Added Sugars: Limit your intake of added sugars, as they contribute to weight gain, tooth decay, and an increased risk of chronic diseases. The American Heart Association recommends limiting added sugars to no more than 6 teaspoons (25 grams) per day for women and 9 teaspoons (36 grams) per day for men.
  • Sources of Added Sugars: Common sources include sugar-sweetened beverages, candy, baked goods, and processed foods. Look for ingredients like high fructose corn syrup, sucrose, glucose, and dextrose.
  • Net Carbs: Some people follow a "net carb" approach, which involves subtracting the grams of fiber from the total carbohydrates. This is a way to estimate the impact of a food on blood sugar levels. However, it's not officially recognized on the Nutrition Facts label.

6. Protein: Building and Repairing​

  • What it is: The amount of protein in a serving.
  • Why it matters: Protein is essential for building and repairing tissues, producing enzymes and hormones, and supporting a healthy immune system.
  • Recommended Daily Intake: The recommended daily intake of protein is about 0.8 grams per kilogram of body weight (or about 0.36 grams per pound).
  • Complete vs. Incomplete Proteins: Complete proteins contain all nine essential amino acids. Animal products (meat, poultry, fish, eggs, dairy) are complete proteins. Plant-based proteins are often incomplete, but you can combine different plant sources (e.g., beans and rice) to get all the essential amino acids.

7. Vitamins and Minerals: Essential Nutrients​

  • What it is: The label lists the percentage of the Daily Value (%DV) for certain vitamins and minerals, including vitamin D, calcium, iron, and potassium.
  • Why it matters: Vitamins and minerals are essential for various bodily functions.
  • Daily Value (DV): The %DV tells you how much of a specific nutrient is in a serving of food, compared to the recommended daily intake.
  • General Guidelines:
    • 5% DV or less is considered low
    • 20% DV or more is considered high
  • Focus on Key Nutrients: Pay attention to nutrients that you may be lacking in your diet. For example, if you don't consume dairy, focus on getting enough calcium from other sources.
  • Vitamins and Minerals are not always absorbed: The human body does not always absorb all the vitamins and minerals from supplements and food sources.

The Ingredient List: What's Really in Your Food?​

The ingredient list is just as important as the Nutrition Facts label. It lists all the ingredients in a food product, in descending order by weight.

  • What it is: A list of all the ingredients in a food product, from most abundant to least abundant.
  • Why it matters: The ingredient list can reveal hidden sugars, unhealthy fats, artificial additives, and allergens.
  • First Few Ingredients: Pay close attention to the first few ingredients, as they make up the largest proportion of the product.
  • Added Sugars: Look for hidden sources of added sugars, such as high fructose corn syrup, sucrose, glucose, dextrose, maltose, and corn syrup.
  • Unhealthy Fats: Avoid products that list partially hydrogenated oil (trans fat) or large amounts of saturated fat (e.g., palm oil, coconut oil) near the top of the list.
  • Artificial Additives: Be aware of artificial colors, flavors, and preservatives. Some people are sensitive to these additives.
  • Allergens: The ingredient list will often highlight common allergens, such as milk, eggs, peanuts, tree nuts, soy, wheat, and fish.
  • "Natural" Doesn't Always Mean Healthy: The term "natural" is often used loosely. It doesn't necessarily mean that the product is healthy or free of artificial ingredients.
  • Short Ingredient Lists are Often Better: Generally, products with shorter ingredient lists are less processed and contain fewer artificial additives.

Understanding Common Label Claims​

Food manufacturers often use claims on their packaging to attract consumers. Here's a breakdown of some common claims:

  • "Low Fat": 3 grams of fat or less per serving.
  • "Reduced Fat": At least 25% less fat than the regular product.
  • "Fat-Free": Less than 0.5 grams of fat per serving.
  • "Low Sodium": 140 mg or less of sodium per serving.
  • "Reduced Sodium": At least 25% less sodium than the regular product.
  • "Sodium-Free": Less than 5 mg of sodium per serving.
  • "Low Sugar": Not defined by the FDA, but generally means the product has a small amount of sugar.
  • "Sugar-Free": Less than 0.5 grams of sugar per serving.
  • "No Added Sugar": No sugars were added during processing. However, the product may still contain naturally occurring sugars.
  • "High Fiber": 5 grams or more of fiber per serving.
  • "Good Source of Fiber": 2.5 to 4.9 grams of fiber per serving.
  • "Whole Grain": Contains at least 51% whole grains by weight per serving.
  • "Organic": Must meet specific standards set by the USDA.
  • "Gluten-Free": Contains less than 20 parts per million (ppm) of gluten.
  • "Natural": As mentioned earlier, this term is often used loosely and doesn't necessarily mean the product is healthy.

Putting It All Together: A Step-by-Step Guide​

Here's a practical approach to reading food labels:

  1. Start with the Serving Size: Determine how much you're actually going to eat.
  2. Check the Calories: Consider the calorie content in relation to your daily needs.
  3. Examine the Fat Content: Pay attention to saturated and trans fats, and aim for more unsaturated fats.
  4. Watch the Sodium: Limit your sodium intake to less than 2,300 mg per day.
  5. Focus on Fiber: Choose foods with a high fiber content.
  6. Limit Added Sugars: Be mindful of the amount of added sugars in the product.
  7. Consider Protein: Ensure you're getting enough protein in your diet.
  8. Check Vitamins and Minerals: Pay attention to nutrients you may be lacking.
  9. Read the Ingredient List: Look for hidden sugars, unhealthy fats, artificial additives, and allergens.
  10. Compare Products: Use food labels to compare different products and choose the healthiest option.

Beyond the Label: Other Considerations​

  • Focus on Whole, Unprocessed Foods: The best way to eat healthy is to prioritize whole, unprocessed foods like fruits, vegetables, lean proteins, and whole grains. These foods often don't have labels, or their labels are very simple.
  • Cook at Home: Cooking at home allows you to control the ingredients and portion sizes.
  • Be Wary of Marketing Tactics: Food manufacturers often use marketing tactics to make their products seem healthier than they actually are.
  • Consult a Registered Dietitian: If you have specific dietary needs or health concerns, consult a registered dietitian for personalized advice.

Conclusion: Empower Yourself with Knowledge​

Reading food labels is a powerful tool that can help you make informed choices and improve your overall health. By understanding the Nutrition Facts label, the ingredient list, and common label claims, you can navigate the grocery store with confidence and choose foods that support your well-being. So, take the time to read those labels – your body will thank you for it!
